Color Mixing

Recipes from Matthews' How to Paint Signs & Sho' Cards (1920). Proportions by part where given. Type to filter.

  • Primaries: red, blue, yellow. Mix two → the third is its strongest contrast (red↔green, blue↔orange, yellow↔purple).
  • Depth cue: yellow advances, red sits still, blue recedes.
  • Add black sparingly — it's very strong. Use more of the light colors than the dark.
Period source — some originals used white lead; those are left out. Use a modern opaque white. Mixing guidance only, not a safety reference.

Emphasis — 4 Ways

Golden rule: something big / small / in-between — same for weight.

  • 1. Size — make the emphasis biggest. Needs contrast; everything else smaller.
  • 2. Weight — make it boldest.
  • 3. Value most important — light-on-dark / dark-on-light. Yellow highest on black, then white; black highest on white. Wrong value ruins a good layout.
  • 4. Placement — reads top→bottom, left→right. Funky order reads like Yoda.

Can't get all three on the emphasis? Value alone can still carry it.

The Alphabets

Rules & Optical Tricks

  • Margins: interior margin must stay less than exterior. Bottom > top for optical center.
  • Curved strokes (O, C, S, b, e…) slightly overlap top & bottom guidelines so they don't read small. B P R D are flat on top.
  • Bars & diagonals a "tick" thinner than stems (Gothic/Phoenix). Bars thicker than stems = distorted (only Western breaks this).
  • Touching: letters & panels either clear-space or intentionally overlap — never just touch (reads as "ran out of room").
  • Movement on the emphasis first. Arc enough to read intentional. Min movement = capitalize first/last letter (~¾ height).
  • Shade/outline must recede — never out-contrast the letter, or you see it but can't read it. Outline ~doubles the price.
  • Panels: weenie / pinched / notched beat a rectangle. A panel = a mini-sign → gets its own margins. Run it edge-to-edge to reclaim size.
  • Counter space matters as much as the letter — keep M/N counters even; prefer a slightly larger counter.
  • Max 3 alphabets per sign. Keep one phrase in one style. Throwaway words → script.
  • Gothic I = a single stem, no serifs. Serifs on a Gothic I = amateur tell.

Spacing ICS 1902

Goal: equal optical space between letters, not equal measured gaps. Shorten extended letters, spread parallel ones. The Letters tab gives these as numbers from your stem.

Full Block & Roman (spur styles):

  • Spur to spur (B–E) — one stroke width between the spurs.
  • Spur to plain (B–O) — ¾ stroke between body/stroke.
  • Plain to plain (O–H) — one full stroke between.
  • Slanting letters (W, Y) — ½ stroke at the spurs.

Egyptian / Half Block / French Roman (plain styles):

  • Parallel-stroke letters — one stroke between.
  • Projecting letters — ½ stroke between.
  • Round to round (O–C) — ¼ stroke.
  • Word space — never less than one full letter (incl. spurs); aim for 1½.
  • Never let letters touch — except shaded, and only if unavoidable.
  • Round–round (OO) condensed may almost touch and still look right; pairs like L–A or J–E read tighter than they measure, so spread them.
  • The trouble letters are the slants (A K V W Y) and projectors (F J L T). A single bad gap makes one word look like two ("like a company of soldiers with a gap").

Letter Spacing — Optical John King

The modern eye-first method: equalize the negative-space area between letters, not the measured gap. There's no formula — trust the eye.

Spacing by the eye

  • Never space mechanically. Equal measured gaps between straight edges look uneven; balance the area of space instead.
  • Compensate for shape. Round (O C G) and open letters carry extra apparent space — tuck them closer. Straight-sided neighbours (M N H I L) set the baseline gap; gauge the rest to match.
  • The A/V/W gauge: set an A, V or W almost touching an adjacent straight-edged letter, then gauge all other spacing to that.
  • Problem combos (L–T, L–Y): the L's foot leaves a big empty pocket — cheeky but permissible to shave a little off the right of the L to soften it.
  • Tight = handcrafted, loose = computer-like. Working digitally, tighten by hand to kill the computerized look.

Double spacing

  • Deliberately wide spacing extends a short word or secondary line without enlarging or stretching the letters — and reads as a design element. Drop dots/diamonds between letters: W · E · L · C · O · M · E.

Layout & Lettering Bond 1937

Showcard design rests on four principles — symmetry, balance, proportion, spacing — that together make "completeness."

Building the layout

  • Four elements: display lines · body lettering · ornament/illustration · borders & margins.
  • Find the "high light." Read the copy, pick the one selling point, emphasise only it. Don't emphasise the obvious (no need to enlarge "Shirts" in a shirt window) — lead with the real news: "New Season's Designs…" beats "Shirts…".
  • Avoid confusion. Too many display lines = disorder. Aim for one punchy headline (+ price) and a subordinate sub-head; contrast carries the read.
  • "Spot" it first — settle the design with rough miniature thumbnail sketches before committing to the card.

Balance & proportion

  • Balance — obvious/symmetrical (two halves mirror) vs occult/"felt" (asymmetrical but balanced). Models: the pyramid (broad base → point) and the lever (4 lb at 1 balances 1 lb at 4 — visual weight × distance from centre). Sense it, don't measure.
  • Proportion — pleasing card ratios 3:5 and 3:8 ("Golden Measure"); avoid mechanical exact 2×/3×; width about ½–⅔ of the length. The copy-mass should echo the card shape; rectangles read better than squares.
  • Subordination — a graded hierarchy; no two elements equal in value/colour/strength or they fight. Repose — quiet, dignified harmony from the right proportion of each part.

Lettering

  • Caps vs lower case: caps for headings/emphasis only; body in lower case. Over-using caps = confusion and harder reading — the fewer words in caps, the more they stand out.
  • Style fits the subject: "Dainty Lingerie" delicate · "Iron Piping" bold · "Modern Furniture" modern · "Antiques" Old English. White highlights make "Iced Soda" icy; haze lines make "Hot" hot.
  • Headlines are the boldest part — the card lives or dies on the headline. For punch they can stagger: letters at angles, uneven heights, falling/overlapping — but keep a pleasing look.
  • Script gives a heading "kick" and dignity; Old English suits Christmas and religious work only.

Shading ICS 1902

Light assumed at 45°. Shade widest midway along a stroke, tapering to nothing at the ends — the common error is too much thickness where it starts/finishes.

Default: bottom & left side

  • Shade bottom-and-left first; learn that before the right. Reasons: more letters (S E C R) shade symmetrically on the left; it's faster; and the strokes pull toward you instead of pushing.
  • Hold the same angle at every point. Show every characteristic point of the letter in the shade. Equal width on all letters — except round letters, where the shade reaches full width only at the thickest point.

The five treatments

  • Block shade — two tones giving the letter thickness: darker under all horizontals, lighter on the side of all verticals.
  • Cast shadow — top of the shade ⅕ of letter-height below the top; thrown 30° to the left, point on the lower-left corner. (Or duplicate the letter as a shadow, ⅕ down, at the regular 45°.)
  • Relief shade — leave a uniform gap between letter and shade, on the shade's angle; gap and shade equal width.
  • Beveled — shade left + bottom from a line through the center of the face. Good on gold (varnish + asphaltum) or silver (varnish + lampblack).
  • Heavy highlight — upper half a light tint, darker shade on the lower half, top edge left white.

The highlight

  • A fine, even line on the edge opposite the shade (top & right of the strokes).
  • Colored letter → gold or silver. Gold letter → cream or white. Silver / aluminum → white only.

From the course:

  • Shade must recede — never out-contrast the letter, or you read the shade-blob, not the word. Keep it low-contrast; let it sit lower than the bottom margin if needed (the eye reads the letters).
  • Outlining ~doubles the job's time. Often unnecessary.

Casual & Slope Gage 1924

Casual / Italic is a sloped Roman, closer to handwriting — made fast off the brush.

  • Pick a slope and keep it. There's no correct angle — but once chosen it must hold through the whole job. Rule the sheet with light parallel slant lines to guide hand and eye. (Slant tool on the Letters tab gives the lean.)
  • One firm stroke per element. Keep the brush moving ahead — up, down, or forward, never backward against the stroke.
  • The swing of the casual lowercase comes from the oval shapes carried through the alphabet — that's where the motion lives.
  • Diagonal caps (A M V W X Y) must not tip past your chosen slope — keep their angles symmetrical to it. On a level word they don't lean to either side.
  • Inclined panel or ribbon: keep letters at a uniform angle, vertical or perpendicular to the panel — don't twist each to follow the surface (ICS).
  • Always keep numbers & prices plain, and give the price the emphasis.

Roman Construction Kynaston (DK) 2024

The beauty of Roman is the simplicity of the letter. Think of letters as architecture — functional first, then decorative. Learn the classics and the rest falls into place.

Built on a 10-point system (cap = 10 points high). Widths in the DK Roman A–Z.

  • Marking out: rule line A/B, then A/C; divide A/C into 10 equal parts; project points 1–10 across to A/B; copy the 10 points onto a set square for fast marking.
  • Everything fits a circle or a square with even proportions throughout.
  • All "straight" lines are drawn slightly concave — never dead-flat. Round letters: compass first, finish freehand, 15° compass offset, 45° stress.
  • Serifs just under 1 point wide; stems 1 point; bars/spurs ½ point.
  • The S — the letter most sign writers struggle with — is two equal circles, the top one ½-point forward of the bottom. Master it; you're judged by it.
  • Curves project slightly past the guides; angled points (N etc.) go just past the guide lines. M & W centres are the same.

Spacing: two straights = 3 points · straight + curve = 2 points · two curves = 1 point. Double spacing is ideal for Roman.

Pen-Written Roman Gage 1924

Show-card Roman written with a flat nib (wide pen). The quickest and easiest alphabet — the pen does the thick/thin work automatically. Use a flat brush for letters taller than ¾″.

Four governing rules

  • Up strokes always thin — pen held so vertical/down-left strokes are full pen width.
  • Down strokes always thick — the full width of the nib.
  • Curves graduate between thin and thick through the arc.
  • Horizontal strokes thin — crossing the nib produces a thin line naturally.

Proportions

  • Thick element ≈ ⅛ of letter height. Thin element ≈ ½ of the thick.
  • Lowercase ≈ half cap height — use the next pen size down.
  • Keep the pen moving forward (up, down, or forward) — never backward against the nibs.

Per-letter notes

  • A M V W X Y — diagonal angles must stay centred; don't let them tip to either side.
  • B P R — semi-circular lobes must merge into the stem smoothly, no bump at the join.
  • C G O Q — circle-based; these need the most practice to keep consistent.
  • S — upper lobe smaller than lower; the accent line runs through the centre. The hardest letter in this style too.
  • N H M — kept wide; adjust pen angle slightly to prevent excess weight on the diagonals.

Paint & Thinner

  • One Shot — oil-based enamel. Skins over; cut the skin close to the can edge, keep chunks out.
  • Mineral spirits — ScreenWrite (RITE) from McLogan's, ~$20/gal. Thins paint, cleans brushes, degreases (aluminum / vehicle / boat — not glass or wood).
  • Reuse thinner forever: dirty wash bucket, let sediment settle, pour off the clear amber, scrape, refill.
  • Red & maroon fade fastest — add hardener to One Shot red, ~2 coats. Brushed fades faster than rolled.

Brush Care

  • End of day: wipe excess paint → work in wash pot → spin out (over trash, low, away from faces) → oil → lay flat.
  • Oil = plain transmission fluid (red) in a labeled jar, into the ferrule. Stops hairs going brittle.
  • Silver tray = cleaned & oiled. Rinse the oil before painting — paint over an oiled brush won't dry.

Layout & Transfer

  • Stabilo All grease pencil — blue 8041 (light surface), white 8052 (dark). Avoid red/green/black/yellow, they stain. Work light.
  • Clean Stabilo after paint dries: water/spit, or 409 for stubborn lines.
  • Pattern: draw → perforate (electro-pounce ~$1k, or pounce wheel) → sand the back lightly to open holes → pounce charcoal (dark) / chalk (light). Or use Saral transfer paper.
  • Aluminum: peel the protected side, wipe with mineral spirits before painting.

Gilding (glass) 1920 method

Water-size gold leaf on glass, step by step (Matthews). The highest branch — master other window work first.

  1. Draw the full design on the outside of the glass (chalk/crayon). Small work: make a pounce pattern.
  2. Clean inside: whiting + water, then wipe with alcohol, polish with tissue. No fingerprints after.
  3. Boil water size: ~1 pt clean water + 4 gelatin capsules (or fish glue the size of a nickel), boil 5 min, strain.
  4. Size only part of the design at a time, starting top-left (if right-handed).
  5. Cut a leaf in half on the book; rub the gilder's tip on your hair, pick up the gold, carry it to the wet glass — it jumps to the size.
  6. Leaf solid over the design; overlap leaves ~¼"; keep the glass wet ahead, but don't flow size over fresh gold.
  7. When dry & bright: brush off loose gold with cotton, then burnish lightly with a clean cotton wad.
  8. Re-size and patch the holes ("holidays"); dry; brush & burnish again. A third gild is sometimes needed.
  9. "Wash" the back with hot size for a high burnish.
  10. Back up the letters with black (Japan). Use a yardstick + hard pencil to scratch straight top/bottom lines in the gold.
  11. When dry, damp-cotton off the surplus gold outside the letters; straighten ragged edges with a razor blade.
  12. Outline / shade as desired; varnish with Spar, extending it slightly past the letters. A few drops of fat oil keeps it elastic.
Silver leaf: ~50% more gelatine in the size, badger/ox-hair tip. Never use silver leaf on wood or exterior — it blackens.
